This site serves as a vehicle for learning and thinking about mathematics,
philosophy, Catholic theology, Scriptural study, Classical and Koine Greek,
Latin, and indirectly, about writing, coding techniques, and tools. It's a
digital [commonplace book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonplace_book), an
amateur-scholar's notebook.

There's an old tradition, found in both the classical and Christian worlds, that
sees study differently. For Aristotle, the highest forms of knowledge were worth
pursuing because they fulfilled something fundamental in human nature. For
Thomas Aquinas, the desire to know is part of our orientation towards truth, and
ultimately towards God. For John Henry Newman, liberal knowledge was valuable
not merely because it was useful, but because it enlarged and harmonized the
mind. And for G. H. Hardy, mathematical ideas, for which "beauty is the first
test", offer a unique, enduring form of knowledge.

One of the most liberating realizations for independent amateur scholars is that
there is no finish line. The subjects into which this digital notebook delves
are effectively all inexhaustible. The goal is not completion but companionship.

Newman wrote in Discourse 5 of *The Idea of a University*: "knowledge is capable
of being its own end." That idea runs against much of modern culture, which
tends to look only at the utility of knowledge. But there is a deep human
satisfaction in pursuing truth, beauty, and wisdom simply because they are worth
knowing. With goodness, truth and beauty are the objective attributes of
reality, and wisdom allows us to intereact with these realities.

This notebook doesn't follow any particular curriculum--it adjusts as it goes.
The greatest danger for self-directed learning is not choosing the wrong path,
but spending so much time planning the journey that the journey itself never
really begins.

The subjects form a coherent whole. Theology asks what is ultimately true. Greek
and Latin open the original sources. The classics explore the human condition as
it's been seen from the start of our collective learning adventure. Mathematics
cultivates clarity and rigour of thought and exposes pure, abstract beauty. They
are not competing subjects so much as different windows onto the same reality
and they combine to generate a rich intellectual life by any standard.

And, perhaps one of the privileges of being at last free of the demands of
earning a living, is that learing like this can be pursued without needing to
justify it to anyone except oneself, and, in a theological sense, perhaps to
God.
